The stakes have never been higher

Digital payment volumes crossed $15 trillion globally in 2025. With scale comes risk — fraudsters, nation-state actors, and opportunistic criminals target every layer of the payment stack. For banks and merchants, securing every transaction is a baseline expectation, not a differentiator.

Layered payment security model

What each layer does

1. Authentication

Before a transaction is submitted, the user's identity must be verified. MFA, biometric checks, and device fingerprinting block account takeover attempts at the gate.

2. Encryption and tokenization

All sensitive data — card numbers, account details, CVVs — is tokenized at capture. The actual PAN never travels through the network; a surrogate token does, rendering intercepts useless.

3. Real-time fraud detection

Every transaction is scored by Clari5Pay's AI engine in under 50ms. Behavioral analytics, network graph analysis, and velocity rules flag anomalies before funds move.

4. Settlement and audit

Cleared transactions land in an immutable, cryptographically signed audit trail. Every decision is logged with full context for regulatory review.

The limits of rule-based systems

Legacy fraud systems rely on static rules — block if amount exceeds threshold, flag if country changes. Fraudsters have long learned to probe these limits, use money mules, and mimic legitimate patterns until the rules become obsolete. AI-powered detection changes the equation: instead of rules, the system builds a model of normal for every customer and merchant, then flags deviations in real time.

AI fraud detection pipeline

The five signals Clari5Pay monitors

  • Velocity anomalies — multiple transactions in a short window from the same account or device
  • Geographic impossibility — a card used in Mumbai and London within 30 minutes
  • Behavioral deviation — spending pattern diverges sharply from the 90-day customer baseline
  • Network graph signals — a recipient account connected to known mule networks
  • Device intelligence — emulators, rooted devices, or mismatched fingerprints

Clari5Pay's AI engine reduces fraud losses by up to 70% compared to rule-based-only systems, while cutting false positives by more than half.

What is PCI DSS?

The Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard is the global baseline for protecting cardholder data. Any business that stores, processes, or transmits credit card information must comply. Non-compliance exposes merchants to fines up to $100,000 per month and potential card network disqualification.

PCI DSS 4.0 compliance journey

Key changes in 4.0

Stronger authentication

All admin access to cardholder data environments now requires MFA without exception. Passwords must meet stricter complexity requirements and rotate at least annually.

Customised approach

Organisations can now demonstrate compliance through a customised implementation rather than prescriptive controls, provided they document the objective and provide evidence of equivalent protection.

Understanding chargebacks

A chargeback occurs when a cardholder disputes a transaction and their bank forcibly reverses the funds. The merchant loses the goods, the transaction amount, and a dispute fee. Chargeback rates above 1% can trigger card network monitoring programmes that restrict payment acceptance.

Chargeback lifecycle

The three types

True fraud

Stolen card used without cardholder knowledge. 3DS2 and real-time AI scoring catch the majority before authorisation.

Friendly fraud

A legitimate customer disputes a charge they actually made — over 70% of all chargebacks. Delivery confirmation, clear billing descriptors, and order confirmation emails are the best defences.

Merchant error

Duplicate charges or failed cancellations. Strong internal controls eliminate most of these entirely.

Two approaches

Tokenization and encryption are the two most widely used techniques for protecting payment data. Both prevent sensitive data from being exposed but achieve this in fundamentally different ways.

Tokenization vs encryption

When to use tokenization

Tokenization is ideal for storing payment references for recurring billing, subscriptions, or one-click checkout. Tokens can be stored safely in your database — without the vault they are worthless.

When to use encryption

Encryption is right for transmitting sensitive data between device and server. TLS 1.3 encrypts data in transit; AES-256 is the standard for data at rest.

Clari5Pay uses both: tokenization replaces PANs at point of capture; end-to-end encryption protects all data in transit. Keys are managed in certified HSMs and rotated on a defined schedule.

What is a money mule?

A money mule is an individual who receives stolen funds and transfers them onward, taking a cut. Mules obscure the money trail between the original fraud and the final beneficiary. Mule recruitment surged in 2025, with criminal networks using social media, job platforms, and dating apps to recruit victims under the guise of remote work or payment processing roles.

Money mule network detection

Why single-account analysis fails

A mule account may look perfectly normal in isolation. The signal is in the network: who is sending money in, who is receiving it out, and whether counterparties are connected to known fraud cases.

Clari5Pay's mule detection has helped partner banks disrupt organised mule networks across Southeast Asia and the Middle East, recovering funds before they exit the banking system.

The problem with 3DS1

The original 3D Secure protocol was notorious for poor user experience — a clunky redirect and cart abandonment rates exceeding 20%. Many merchants disabled it entirely. 3DS2 solves this with risk-based authentication: most transactions are authenticated silently, without any user interaction.
